Nokia Developer Summit – Qt Ubiquity

When Nokia acquired the Norwegian-based Trolltech just over a year ago, some had already seen the future. Now it's official: Qt is the new development platform for Nokia devices. You read it right: Qt is a development platform and not only a UI framework as many have wrongly come to think. Tools like the QtCreator, QtDesigner and QtLinguist will be the new standard for Nokia development. For those still unaware of this Qt-wave, Qt is basically a way of doing common things in an easier and cross-platform way. Nokia made sure to communicate this over and over again.

Qt will continue to enjoy its strong position in cross-platform development for desktops but its adoption on Maemo, S60 and S40 devices will grow steadily. Except for S40, all other devices already enjoy quite stable Qt support. For our S40 friends, Qt may take a bit longer to appear since there are some very challenging memory consumption problems that make it difficult to port to such low-end phones.

Among many roadmap items revealed for Qt, there are extended support for embedded devices (not limited to mobile phones in any extent), multi-touch support, a new animation framework and expansion of its open-source model allowing contributors to submit patches. Declarative UI is also a great feature that is planned for the future and under very active research and development. If well-designed, this will be a potential break-through.

Nokia's next big thing for Qt though, is its Qt-like service API layer. This will allow cross-platform access to mobile devices services for S40, S60 and Maemo. Nokia is also actively trying to collaborate with other device manufacturers in order to have a standardized Qt API that would work across multiple vendors.

Qt's development approach prioritizes a hybrid programming model where multiple technologies can be mashed up together to achieve desired results. This means that Qt can be used for combining the new Webkit technology, C++ or even ECMAScript. Nokia's highlight here was for an easy-to-develop, cheaper environment when compared to current Symbian development.
