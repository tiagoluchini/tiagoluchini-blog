Nokia Developer Summit – Symbian Foundation and the future of S60

It's sad to say but Symbian Foundation provided the dullest presentations during the Summit as a whole. Most of the talk was around its pure open-source philosophy and the creation of an active community effort to provide a healthy development ecosystem. It remains to be seen whether this will work and, most importantly, when this will happen. By now, only 4% of the source code is under EPL (Eclipse Public License). The plan is to achieve 100% by June 2010 but even the Foundation proponents seemed to doubt that.

One of the main impediments to full open-source has been the legal front. Things like the Java Virtual Machine are not in the platform even though they are traditionally provided by device manufacturers. In this Java case, Sun charges for manufacturers using their implementation and, as such, exposing that as open-source is not legally possible. Symbian Foundation is strongly considering replacing Sun's implementation with some other open-sourced one if the legal battles prolong too much. Not very bright news for our J2ME fellows and, it's important to mention, this was the only mention to J2ME during the event.

There will be two licensing models in place for Symbian: EPL (Eclipse Public License) for the community and SFL (Symbian Foundation License) for Symbian partners only. This dual license model is expected to allow a very business-friendly ecosystem.

It has been decided that the S60 naming convention will be dropped and the next release will be simply called Symbiam^2. Future releases will follow this numbering progressively. Symbian^3 has on its roadmap improved UI and better Internet connectivity. Symbian^4 will be the first release to be fully ran by the community-driven effort and, as such, the roadmap is still open for community developers to create. It's a big recommendation though that Qt will be an integral part of Symbian^4.

Maybe one of the biggest revelations was that the development tools for traditional S60 programming are also being made open source. This will include tools such as Carbide but also many others currently just used internally by OEMS and Nokia.

Another important heads up is that the new Symbian will have a revamped build system that will be better connected to Linux's standard make tools. This might mean some learning needed for our Symbian friends.

Symbian Foundation is also working with Texas Instrument to be able to provide an open hardware implementation. The latest device is called TI Zoom 2 and is already available.

The whole Symbian Foundation and its open-source movement is targeted to make Symbian coders' lives easier by providing means of community-driven peer review of platform code, bug fixing and submission of patches. Submitting extensions to the platform code is also a possibility in the future.
